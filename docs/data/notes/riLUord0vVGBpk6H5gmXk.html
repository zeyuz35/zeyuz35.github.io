Austrian

One of the first schools, and is the closest to microeconomic theory. It is considered to be heterodox (not widely accepted).

It argues that all social phenomena are the exclusive result from the motivations and actions of individuals only.

Keynesian

Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply Curves - what is usually covered in a first year Macroeconomics course.

Monetarism (Friedman)

This has fallen out of favour as Central Banks found it harder to target total money supply, as opposed to interest rates.

This has also become unpopular politically, as central banks needed to create recessions in order to control inflation.

New Classical

The introduction of rational expectations to macroeconomics.

Previously, agents were assumed to simply make decisions based on historical data. However, rational agents are more sophisticated and instead looked at monetary policy announcements, etc to make decisions.

When this was accounted for, monetary policy was shownt o have only limited impact, presumably because agents are already taking any announcements into account.

This eventually led to the development of the "real business cycle" models. However, this model explained recessions with changes in technology instead of changes in the markets, and some have said that this is implausible.

New Keynesian

An attempt at providing microeconomic foundations for justifying Keynesian Economics.

New Keynesian economics tries to justify demand management (monetary and fiscal policy) via the introduction of microeconomic foundations leading to market imperfections.

This is to date, the most accepted, and seems to have the widest consensus among economists.
