Separate section for Great Moderation.

There is no precise date for the Great Moderation, apart from mid 1980s. Wikipedia puts this as 1985 rather arbitrarily.

Importantly, the source of a lack of decrease in volatility is mostly observed from only a few key macroeconomic variables. By definition, the Great Moderation is associated with major economic variables such as real GDP, monthly payroll employment, and unemployment rates. Real wages, consumer prices remained stable (stable inflation). Interest rates reversed their upward trend and started to fall. 

Some sources put this as 1984 (McConell Paper). Others provide some evidence for 1982 (see Bruce Hansen). More precise evidence for Great Moderation as a structural break tends to focus volatility in the business cycle, as measured by output. There are further attempts at disaggregating GDP into various components, but in general the focus is still on economic output. 

Indeed, although there is a marked increase in inflation staibility, empirical evidence for this as part of the Great Moderation in terms of a structural break is weak. 

Unfortunately this period also saw large increases in household debt and increasing economic polarization. 

Reconciling the evidence:
When looking at the marginal R squared values, it seems that a max of 5 factors is reasonable.
However, most of the information lies in the first factor (labour market, interst rates, consumption and output). The second and third factors mostly lod onto interest rates.

The fourth loads onto output and consumption. Most of the price series load onto the fifth factor. A bit of a conundrum.

Possible Explanation:
The variability of inflation is very highly correlated with output volatility since the mid 1970s. (see Blanchard and Simon). From this perspective, the first factor may be sufficient to capture the volatility of the price series. Indeed, although the output factor and price factor are not correlated (by construction), their volatilities are highly correlated.

The price factor responded very strongly to the first oil shock a few years earlier than the output factor.

Later, the magnitude of the responses of the price and output factor are different, but the timing is similar. 

If you restrict the sample to be after the mid 1970s, the voltaility (square) of the price and output factors are more correlated, and have peaks at the same time. However, the magnitude is different, which is perhaps why they seem less correlated than they actually are.

Regardless of when exactly this happened, it seems it is quite distinct from the late 1970s. This is more closely associated with the Iranian Revolution which in turn lead to the Second Oil Shock. 

The price of Oil did not return to pre crisis levels until the mid 1980s, again coinciding with Great moderation.

Bernanke's (in)famous speech attributes the Great Moderation to three sources:
  - structural change in the economy
  - improved economic policies
  - good luck

Given that this was given in 2004, comapratively just before the GFC, in retrospect it is considered to be very prematurely self-congratulatory in tone.

Some provide the persepctive that the Great Moderation was simply a tradeoff between risk and stability. That is, the Fed chose to stabilize the economy in the short term in a trade off for potentially larger, long term risk (GFC).
